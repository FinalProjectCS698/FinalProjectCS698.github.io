Automated Classification of Insects Using Wingbeat Frequency

Traditional insect population surveying methods, involving labor-intensive approaches like light traps and pitfall traps, often in remote areas, lead to intrusive and time-consuming data collection processes. Remote sensing, a non-invasive, efficient, and long-term monitoring technique, has emerged as a promising alternative. It streamlines the observation-to-identification process, revolutionizing biological surveying. Classification challenges exist due to overlapping wingbeat frequencies within genera and families, prompting exploration of discriminative factors like body-wing ratio and melanin content for improved precision. Large-scale insect population surveys benefit agriculture, healthcare, and conservation, offering tailored pest control, vector control, and ecological impact assessment, ensuring ecosystem preservation. Using wingbeat frequencies for identification does not explicitly require machine learning, as it possible to isolate the frequency and categorize it with empirical data by hand, but, using machine learning can improve efficiency and speed of the process. Additionally, the data is also noisy, making it take more time for one to isolate and identify potential signals. The use of machine learning for this task can help mitigate this. While this does require the labeling of some data, labeling 5-10% of the dataset and employing a model to learn based upon this, takes less time than labeling the entire dataset without the utilization of a model.
